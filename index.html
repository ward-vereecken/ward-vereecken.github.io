Biggest project to date

Thesis project: An AI modeling of a psychological concept called Choice Blindness through online study.

Final score:

17/20 (85%)

Each day, a person makes hundreds to thousands of choices. But what if these choices turn out to be easily manipulated? Choice blindness is the paradigm in which a person is blind for their own choice, mere seconds after making it. This project aimed to study the influence of face and landscape image properties and similarities on choice blindness, through the means of an online study. The relationship between choice blindness and personal preference, confidence, and option types was also investigated. Multiple scene properties were investigated for correlations with choice blindness chances. To this end, colour histograms, colourfulness, contrast and sharpness values were extracted. Texture vectors were also extracted as local binary pattern histograms, facial features shapes were extracted using a neural net model and a busyness metric was defined and used to determine image busyness and its relationship with choice blindness. Next to this, three image similarity measures were defined and inspected for correlations with choice blindness chances: global similarity, defined by low-level image features, GIST similarity, defined by the spatial layout of the image, and neural net similarity, a black box algorithm. These results were collected using an online website in the form of a psychological study, built using HTML and Javascript. Finally, results were thoroughly analyzed and many interesting conclusions and prediction models were derived using Big Data techniques in Python.

Computer Vision: Real-Time Museum image-based locator

In this project, a program was created which localizes a person in the Museum of Fine Arts in Ghent through videos taken by this person. To achieve this, said video footage is processed frame by frame. Potential paintings are detected and extracted from each frame, which are then fed to a Support Vector Machine classifier. Confirmed paintings are matched against a pre-initialized database of known paintings using SIFT feature extraction. Calculated matching scores are then passed to a Hidden Markov Model, which then statistically determines the person's location in the museum.
